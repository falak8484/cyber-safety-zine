Chapter 1
How to spot a phishing email 🎣

Phishing emails pretend to be someone you trust — your bank, Netflix, even your boss. Here's a real example (annotated!) so you can train your eye.

From: security@paypa1-alert.com🚩 Fake domain! Paypal uses @paypal.com
To: you@email.com
Subject: URGENT: Your account will be suspended in 24 hours!🚩 Urgency is a classic pressure tactic

Dear Valued Customer,

We have detected suspicious activity🚩 Vague threat — they don't name the activity on your account. Your account will be permanently suspended🚩 More scare tactics to rush you unless you verify your information immediately.

Click below to confirm your identity and enter your password and card number🚩 Legit companies never ask for this by email:

http://paypa1-secure-login.xyz/verify?id=38291🚩 Sketchy URL! Hover before you click — always.

PayPal Security Team
This email was auto-generated. Do not reply.

Red flags spotted in that email:
Fake sender domain
Urgency & threats
Vague "suspicious activity"
Asks for password/card
Sketchy URL
No personal greeting
01
Check the sender's email
Real companies email from their actual domain. "paypa1-alert.com" ≠ paypal.com. Look for swapped letters!
02
Hover links before clicking
Hover over any link and look at the real URL in your browser's status bar. If it looks weird, don't click!
03
Urgency = manipulation
"Act NOW or lose everything!" is a pressure tactic. Take a breath. Real businesses give you time.
04
When in doubt, go direct
Don't click email links. Open your browser and go directly to the website yourself.

Use a passphrase

4+ random words strung together. Long, memorable, and incredibly hard to crack.

🔐

Use a password manager

Bitwarden or 1Password remembers everything. One strong password to rule them all.

🚫

Never reuse passwords

If one site gets breached, all your accounts with that password are compromised.

📱

Turn on 2FA everywhere

Two-factor auth = even if they get your password, they still can't log in.

Chapter 3
What to do if you're hacked 🚨

Don't panic — but do act fast. Here's your step-by-step response plan, ordered by urgency.

01

Change your passwords immediately

Start with email (your master key), then banking, then everything else. Use a new, unique password for each.

DO NOW
02

Enable two-factor authentication

Turn on 2FA on every account that supports it. SMS is okay, an authenticator app is better.

DO NOW
03

Check haveibeenpwned.com

Enter your email to see which breaches have exposed your data. This tells you which accounts need urgent attention.

DO NOW
04

Tell your bank if financial data was exposed

Call them directly (not via email). They can freeze accounts and issue new cards before damage is done.

DO SOON
05

Scan your devices for malware

Run a full scan with Malwarebytes (free version is great). Do this before logging into anything important.

DO SOON
06

Warn your contacts

If your email or social accounts were compromised, hackers may have sent phishing messages to your contacts. Give them a heads-up!

Bonus
Quick wins for better security ✨

Small habits that make a big difference. Steal all of these.

📶

Avoid public Wi-Fi

Or use a VPN if you must. Coffee shop networks can be sniffed by anyone on them.

🔒

Lock your devices

Auto-lock after 1 minute. Use a PIN, not a pattern — patterns leave smudges.

🔄

Keep software updated

Updates patch security holes. Delaying them = leaving your windows open.

🗄️

Back up your data

3-2-1 rule: 3 copies, 2 media types, 1 offsite (cloud). Ransomware can't hurt what it can't reach.

👁️

Review app permissions

Does your flashlight app need your contacts? No. Audit your apps every few months.

📧

Use an email alias

SimpleLogin or Fastmail aliases keep your real email private. Burn the alias if you get spam.
